# When to Use This Product Brainstorming Skill

## Core Use Cases

This skill is designed for **structured product ideation** when you want ideas that are:
- **User-outcome-focused** (not feature-first or CRUD-based)
- **Diverse** (avoiding obvious, clustered solutions)
- **Evaluable** (ideas you can actually prioritize and ship)

### Best-fit situations:

1. **Feature exploration or direction setting** — You know the problem exists but aren't sure what to build. Example: "We see drop-off in Week 2 of onboarding, but don't know if it's UX friction, unmet expectation, or something else."

2. **Reframing a stalled problem** — The team has been brainstorming the same angle for weeks. A constraint card (10x, design for the opposite, remove the screen) breaks the gridlock.

3. **Balancing shipping mindset with UX rigor** — You need ideas that are feasible and valuable, not just pretty or novel.

4. **Cross-functional alignment** — PMs, designers, and engineers brainstorm together; the structured prompts (HMW, JTBD forces, empathy mapping) ensure everyone speaks the same language.

5. **Narrowing from many possibilities** — You've got a long list and need to pick 3–5 directions with explicit criteria (value/effort, RICE, viability/desirability/feasibility).

## Problem Statement Examples That Work Well

### ✅ Good fit (vague but solvable; real user problem):

**1. Onboarding friction**
> "New users complete their first project 40% of the time, but we see a sharp drop-off after project 1. Data suggests they get stuck on file export or template choice. We don't know if it's UX confusion, education gap, or misaligned expectations."

**2. Feature discoverability**
> "Power users love our advanced filtering, but only 5% of users discover it. Casual users say 'I wish I could do X' when they actually can, in a menu three levels deep."

**3. Workflow inefficiency**
> "Content teams are spending 30 min per video manually configuring formatting. They want consistency across 100+ videos but don't want to repeat work."

**4. Retention gap (specific cohort)**
> "Free trial users who invite one teammate have a 70% conversion rate. Solo users convert at 8%. We don't know if it's collaboration value, social proof, or something else."

**5. Accessibility or inclusion**
> "We get requests from neurodivergent users about our color-heavy UI and rapid notifications causing overwhelm. Our current design assumes quiet focus; reality is messy."

**6. Cross-product UX debt**
> "We have 3 design systems in production (legacy, design 2.0, new modular kit). Teams can't agree on buttons and spacing. Users experience jarring inconsistency."

**7. Adoption barrier (specific use case)**
> "SMB customers love the core feature but rarely adopt our automation layer. They say 'it's too technical' or 'we don't have time to set it up.' Support can't scale onboarding."

**8. Engagement drop (time-based)**
> "Email marketing: open rates drop 50% after week 3. Clicks drop 60%. We're not sure if it's fatigue, irrelevant content, or timing."

---

### ❌ Not a great fit (too solved; too vague; not a real problem):

- **Too solved:** "We need to add a button." (This is already a solution; skip to design.)
- **Too vague:** "Make the product better." (Use the 6Ws prompt first to tighten.)
- **Not real data:** "I think users might want..." (Gut feeling, no evidence. Validate first.)
- **Too broad, no user lens:** "How do we grow?" (Is the problem user acquisition, retention, monetization, or something else?)

## What Makes a Problem Statement Ready for This Skill

A good problem statement includes:
- **Who** (user type, segment, or cohort)
- **What** (observable behavior or friction point)
- **Context** (when, where, why they're doing this)
- **Why it matters** (business metric or user outcome affected)
- **What we don't know** (hypothesis gap, not assumption)

**Example ready statement:**

> "Senior marketers at B2B SaaS companies (10–50 person teams) spend 4–6 hours per week manually syncing campaign data between our tool and their CRM. They want 'one source of truth' but our sync is fragile—it breaks 2–3 times per month, and they lose trust. This affects feature adoption (we're not used for critical workflows) and Net Retention (we're seen as an add-on, not core). We don't know if they'd trade data freshness for reliability, or if a simpler interface would reduce manual checks."

This statement is ready for:
- **How Might We** framing: "How might we help senior marketers trust their data pipeline?"
- **Constraint cards**: "Design for someone with zero technical setup knowledge" or "10x their confidence"
- **JTBD forces**: Map Push (current pain), Pull (what they want), Anxiety (what stops them), Habit (what keeps them in the old way)

---

## Quick Workflow for This Skill

1. **Bring a loose problem** → Skill tightens it via 6Ws if needed
2. **Convert to How Might We** → Opens up ideation
3. **Generate ideas with diversity** (across UX, tech, business, edge-case lenses)
4. **Evaluate with explicit criteria** → Narrow to top 3–5
5. **Optional:** Use thinking-partner mode to dig deeper into one direction

---

**TL;DR:** Use this skill when you have a real user problem (not a solution), want diverse and evaluable ideas, and need a product engineer with UX sensibility to help you think it through.
